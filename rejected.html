Rejected Anti-Patterns

What we explicitly DID NOT pick. Norm/Daytrip/Athena Calderone/Heidi Caillier/Mahno/Reath/Justina Blakeney + modern farmhouse.

What we explicitly REJECTED. These designers and styles are NOT the canon. Knowing the boundaries is as important as knowing the anchors. Each rejection is reasoned, not arbitrary.

Norm Architects — too austere

Norm Architects — austere project 1
Pure light, white walls, minimal to the point of clinical.
rejected_01
Norm Architects — austere project 2
Beautiful but cold. Scandinavian winter sun, not California morning.
rejected_02
Why rejected: Norm's austere work is the platonic ideal of cold Japandi. The owners explicitly want WARM. Cathie Hong's warmth-from-wood beats Norm's purity-from-restraint every time.

Daytrip Studio — dark London modern

Daytrip Studio — fumed oak floors
Dark-stained walnut/oak floors. Direct opposite of light-oak-wide-plank.
rejected_03
Why rejected: Daytrip's hallmark is dark-stained walnut floors + dark moody palettes — directly opposite our locked spec. Beautiful work but wrong temperature for 1490.

Athena Calderone — Brooklyn classic dark

Athena Calderone — navy-plaster Brooklyn
Saturated walls + dark moody atmosphere.
rejected_04
Athena Calderone — family room
Tufted velvet + jewel tones + classic-dark aesthetic.
rejected_05
Why rejected: Athena's Brooklyn townhouse aesthetic = dark, moody, classic with jewel tones. The opposite of California Modern. Her tufted velvet sofas alone disqualify the direction.

Heidi Caillier — cottage traditional

Heidi Caillier — Ballard Cottage dining
Cottage traditional — beadboard, classic trim, dark accent paint.
rejected_06
Why rejected: Heidi's cottage work uses shiplap, beadboard, board-and-batten — direct contradictions of our kill list. Note: Inverness Cottage was the original anchor signal but we've since refined past it.

Mahno residential — textile maximalist

Why rejected: Mahno's work is gorgeous but stacks pattern-on-pattern textiles. We want restraint — ONE color accent per room, never layered patterns. No image included; the principle is "no pattern stacking" rather than rejection of a specific Mahno project.

Reath Design — saturated jewel-tone

Reath Design — Holmby House play room
Saturated jewel-tone palette. Terracotta walls + navy ceilings = us with the volume on 11.
rejected_07
Why rejected: Reath leans into terracotta walls, navy ceilings, emerald accents — saturated jewel-tone palette. We're cream + plaster + ONE muted accent, never saturated.

Justina Blakeney / Jungalow — Coachella boho

Justina Blakeney / Jungalow
Maximalist plant + pattern + macramé + dreamcatchers + kilim revival.
rejected_08
Why rejected: Maximalist plant + pattern + macramé + dreamcatchers + kilim revival. We have plants (cat-safe palms) but in restraint. The whole boho-Coachella vocabulary is on the kill list.

Modern Farmhouse (genre)

Why rejected: Shiplap + barn doors + black metal grid windows + farmhouse sink + chicken wire + "live laugh love." Not us. Not even close. This is the most-common Atlanta default we're actively avoiding.

The reconciled kill list

Across all rejected anchors, the visual elements we will NEVER use:
  • Dark-stained or dark walnut floors
  • Beadboard, shiplap, board-and-batten walls in public rooms
  • Walnut as PRIMARY cabinetry (accent permitted)
  • Open shelves carrying matched-set commercial dishware
  • Saturated wall paint (terracotta, navy, emerald) — single-accent textile is the only color delivery
  • Gilt mirrors, antlers, taxidermy, William Morris wallpaper, dreamcatchers, macramé walls
  • Chrome, polished nickel, exposed Edison cages, crystal, dark bronze (light finishes only — lacquered brass + matte black)
  • Glass coffee tables on chrome legs, lucite, mirrored furniture
  • Espresso / mahogany / cherry / pickled-whitewash wood finishes
  • High-saturation kilim revival rugs, pattern-on-pattern textile stacking
  • Shiplap with barn doors with black metal grid ("modern farmhouse")
  • Tufted furniture (sofas, headboards)
  • Patterned upholstery (florals, plaids, stripes)
